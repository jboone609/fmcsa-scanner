## Introduction

Building and maintaining compliant driver qualification files has always been a challenge for companies. From incomplete files to outdated documents, errors in this process account for **over 30 percent of common FMCSA violations.**

And now with offsite audits, the risks are even greater. If you get called for an offsite audit, you'll only have **48 hours** to turn over your compliance documentation (instead of two weeks) — and all documents must be submitted in a digital format.

With this in mind, converting to digital driver files is no longer optional. If you want to stay compliant and audit-ready, you must have a digital compliance solution in place.

## What Is Regulation Part 391?

Regulation Part 391 helps ensure that all federally-regulated motor carriers are qualified to be on our nation's roadways — in terms of their physical health, professional experience, and motor vehicle record.

These regulations apply to any carrier, no matter how many drivers they employ, that operates a vehicle involved in interstate commerce that:

- Weighs at least 10,001 pounds
- Carries more than 9 passengers
- Transports materials deemed hazardous under the Hazardous Materials Transportation Act

> **Note for owner-operators:** You must serve as both the boss and an employee for your business. This means you must perform the duties of a carrier by keeping detailed records on your drivers and doing follow-up screenings and regular tests as required. You must also follow all the same rules for yourself as a driver.

**Files must be kept for the duration of a driver's employment with your company, plus three years thereafter.**

## The Driver Qualification File (DQF)

The information obtained through the hiring process is compiled into two separate files:

### Driver Qualification File Must Contain:

- DOT-Compliant Application
- A Motor Vehicle Report for every state the driver held a CDL or permit in the past three years
- A road test certificate or photocopy of the driver's CDL
- A valid Medical Examiner's Certificate
- Verification that the medical examiner used is on the NRCME registry
- A Skill Performance Evaluation Certificate (if applicable)

### Safety Performance History (SPH) Must Contain:

- A copy of the driver's written authorization to contact previous employers about their alcohol and controlled substances history
- A copy of all responses received from previous DOT-regulated employers, or your record of attempts to contact them

## The Move to Digital Files

Now that offsite audits are becoming the FMCSA's go-to method of conducting both safety audits and compliance reviews, it's becoming mandatory that carriers move from paper-based files to a digital filing solution.

The FMCSA now requires carriers to submit files electronically during an offsite audit — and **these files must be submitted within 48 hours.** For carriers still maintaining paper files, this means they must audit, scan, and upload all requested documentation to the auditor within two days. This can be a burdensome process that gets increasingly difficult as the driver count grows.

While offsite audits grew 400 percent between 2019 and 2020, 50 percent of carriers are still maintaining paper files. Over 40 percent of carriers surveyed by Foley indicated they don't feel prepared for the new offsite audit process.

### Digital Files = Increased Security

Due to the sensitive information that is contained in the Safety Performance History file, it must be kept in a secure location where only those who are involved in the employment decision can access it. Failure to protect the security of the files can lead to fines from the FMCSA.

Using a secure, online environment to maintain your driver files meets this need. An online compliance environment also protects files from catastrophic damage in the event of a fire, flood, or other disaster.

## Required File Management

To maintain files that are compliant with Regulation Part 391, there are a few key items you must track and update for the duration of each driver's employment.

### Medical Certificates

All drivers must have a DOT medical evaluation at least once every **24 months** (or as often as the medical examiner deems necessary) to ensure they are physically able to drive a commercial motor vehicle. Once this physical is complete, the updated medical certificate must be kept in the Driver Qualification File.

Conditions that require more frequent medical examinations include: hypertension, heart disease, diabetes, and sleep apnea.

### Variance Renewals

If your driver has a physical impairment that affects their ability to operate a commercial motor vehicle safely, they're required to have a state-issued "variance" that must be carried with them whenever they're driving a CMV. If a driver has impaired or missing limbs, they must have a special type of variance known as a Skill Performance Evaluation (SPE).

All variances (including SPEs) need to be renewed periodically and expiration dates must be tracked alongside the driver's medical certificate.

### Medical Certificate Violations

In 2020, over **54,000 drivers** were cited for not having a valid medical certificate during roadside inspections — making it one of the most common driver violations that year. A single violation for a missing, altered, or falsified medical certificate often exceeds **$2,000.**

To prevent hefty fines or out-of-service orders, it's important to keep a close eye on medical certificate and variance expiration dates to ensure that drivers have enough time to schedule a medical evaluation or submit their variance application before their certificate expires.

### License Expiration Dates

In order to legally operate a motor vehicle, drivers must have a valid license and all required endorsements and/or classes must be active. It is important to track expiration dates carefully to ensure that no one with an expired license gets behind the wheel.

How long a CDL is valid depends on where it was issued — each state has its own licensing laws. For example, CDLs must be renewed every 4 years in Connecticut, and every 8 years in Ohio.

In 2020, driving with an expired CDL was one of the top violations, amounting in fines of approximately $2,000 per occurrence. **98% of drivers with an expired CDL were taken off the road.**

### Annual Driving Records

Every year, you must obtain an updated Motor Vehicle Report (MVR) for each driver to ensure they are qualified to drive a commercial motor vehicle. This MVR will show a record of all moving violations, accidents, and license suspensions and revocations. During this process, you must consider:

- Did the driver violate any hazardous materials regulations?
- Did the driver violate any motor vehicle laws that show a general disregard for public safety? (This may include drug and alcohol violations, speeding, or reckless driving.)

This annual MVR must be placed in the Driver Qualification File along with a note showing the name of the person who performed the annual review and the date the review occurred.

## Converting to Digital Files

### Step 1: Identify the Right Digital Solution

Creating digital driver qualification files could be as simple as scanning them into a computer — but it's not a solution that is secure or reliable, and it can't be easily accessed by others in the organization working remotely.

For best results, look for a full digital compliance solution that is hosted online in a secure environment and that provides automated alerts to keep your files compliant.

### Step 2: Audit Paper Files for Compliance

Before you begin converting to digital driver files, fully audit your paper files to make sure they are compliant. A fully automated compliance solution will identify and alert you to missing documents — so if you miss something during the initial audit, you'll be quickly alerted to any issues once you begin the conversion process.

### Step 3: Upload the Required Documentation

Depending on your compliance provider, they may help with the actual conversion of your files. Through this process, due dates for CDLs and medical cards are entered into the system and tracked so that you'll receive an alert each time an update is due.

### Step 4: Switch to a Digital DOT-Compliant Application

A mobile-friendly, DOT-compliant application can be filled out by drivers on any device. They can also upload pictures of their current CDL and medical card directly to your platform account right from their mobile phone.

These documents provide everything needed to run DOT-required background checks and build a compliant driver qualification file for all new drivers — with very little action on your part.

## Reducing Risk with MVR Monitoring

Although employers are only required to run an MVR annually, it has become standard practice to keep close tabs on their drivers' MVRs throughout the year. Enrolling each employee in an **MVR Monitoring program** solves this problem by providing you with new information as it becomes available. This program continuously monitors each employee's record and pulls an updated report as soon as new information is detected. In some states, this occurs immediately; in others, information is updated monthly.

MVR Monitoring also keeps you up to date on the status of each driver's medical certificate and fulfills your annual requirement to run an updated MVR on each driver you employ.
