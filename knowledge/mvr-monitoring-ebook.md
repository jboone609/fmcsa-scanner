# How an MVR Monitoring Program Can Help Protect Your Business

As a Department of Transportation-regulated business, you're well-accustomed to the process of pulling annual Motor Vehicle Reports (MVRs) for every driver you employ. Simply put, you must do so to meet federal recordkeeping requirements.

Still, this process doesn't just check a box to ensure your compliance as a motor carrier—it is an important step to make sure that safe, qualified drivers are behind the wheel. This ultimately helps protect your business, your drivers, and other motorists.

The only problem: What happens when one of your drivers gets a DUI or commits a serious traffic or safety violation a day, week, or month after you pull their annual report? For a growing number of carriers, the solution is **MVR Monitoring**—automated programs that actively monitor the MVRs for CDL drivers on an ongoing basis.

> The National Safety Council cites NCCI data: motor vehicle crashes are the most costly lost-time workers' compensation claims, averaging $73,559 per claim filed in 2015 and 2016.

## How Do MVR Monitoring Programs Work?

MVR Monitoring keeps tabs on drivers by constantly monitoring their driving records and looking for new information. As soon as there is any change, or "hit," found on a driver's record, an updated MVR is pulled and evaluated for accuracy. Employers are immediately alerted to any "hit" and receive the updated MVR for review.

These "hits" could include:
- Accidents, DUIs, speeding tickets, or other motor vehicle violations
- CDL suspensions or revocations
- Safety violations
- Medical certificate downgrades
- Positive changes, such as CDL or medical certificate renewals

As an added benefit, enrollment in an MVR Monitoring program often fulfills the DOT's annual MVR requirement.

### What Information You'll See

MVR Monitoring provides information about a driver's:
- Speeding violations
- DWI convictions
- License suspensions/revocations/expirations
- Medical certificate downgrades

## Why MVR Monitoring is Important

Unsafe drivers are a huge liability:
- The commercial fleet accident rate is **20 percent**
- The average cost to a motor carrier when one of their trucks is in an accident is **$6,000**
- That amount grows to an average of **$150,000** if injuries are involved

MVR Monitoring can help prevent up to **34 percent** of common FMCSA violations by helping you catch glaring violations, such as a suspended CDL, and monitor for unsafe driving patterns.

- Moving violations (speeding, reckless driving, improper lane changes, failure to yield, etc.)
- Driving with a suspended or revoked license
- Not having a current medical certificate
- Failing to use/improper placement of warning devices
- Failing to use a seat belt
- Using a hand-held device
- Having an unauthorized passenger on board

The cost of these violations can run into the thousands of dollars per infraction, not including lost time, increased insurance premiums, and damage to your CSA score and safety rating.

## Not Monitoring Your Drivers? It Could Cost You

As a motor carrier, you are prohibited from permitting a driver to operate a CMV on your behalf if you **know or should have known** that the driver has been disqualified from driving or has otherwise lost their driving privileges.

Regulated drivers have a duty to self-report disqualifying convictions to their employer within 30 days of the conviction date. However, according to the American Association of Motor Vehicle Administrators, **fewer than 80 percent of drivers** let their employers know when they lose their qualification to drive.

This means over 20 percent of your drivers may be putting you at risk of noncompliance. A motor carrier who keeps a disqualified driver on the road faces fines of **nearly $6,000 per violation**—and in the event of an accident, the consequences are far greater.

## 4 Cases That Highlight the Need for MVR Monitoring

**Armijo, Estate of v. Werner Enterprises, Inc. (2019)**
A wrongful death case following a fatal head-on collision. The jury found Werner Enterprises negligent in hiring disqualified drivers and placing them behind the wheel. Verdict: **$40,500,000**.

**Bohne v. Enviroclean (2007)**
After a truck accident, the driver tested positive for crack cocaine. Background checks would have revealed prior drug convictions and a suspended license. Verdict: **in excess of $20 million**.

**Amparo v. Ayala et al. (2019)**
Employer failed to investigate the driver's record during his employment. A negligent entrustment theory was upheld. Verdict: **$15,000,000**.

**Copeland v. Jones et al. (2019)**
The trucking company failed to conduct an adequate background check to discover a dangerous driving history. The jury found the trucking company **75% negligent**. Verdict: **$1,400,000** (including $1,000,000 in punitive damages).

## What You Can Expect From an MVR Monitoring Program

1. **MVR monitoring can act as a deterrent** — Employees who know their driving records are being monitored are more likely to avoid reckless behaviors.
2. **MVR monitoring is very inexpensive** — Only a few dollars per month per driver. You're only charged for a full MVR pull when a new "hit" is detected.
3. **MVR monitoring can help lower insurance premiums** — More and more insurance companies run MVR checks as part of their underwriting process.
4. **MVR monitoring can be bundled with other services** — It complements social media monitoring, criminal monitoring, and random drug testing.
5. **MVR monitoring protects your employees, others on the road, and your brand** — It helps avoid bad press and expensive negligent retention lawsuits.

## Bonus: Motor Vehicle Reports — Frequently Asked Questions

### What is an MVR Background Check?
An MVR background check is a report of a person's driving history, including license expiration, status, license class, endorsements, restrictions, traffic violations, accidents, vehicular crimes, suspensions, and even unpaid parking tickets.

### Is an MVR report a consumer report?
Yes. Employers must comply with the Fair Credit Reporting Act, including disclosure, authorization, and adverse action procedures.

### How far back does an MVR report go?
This varies by state. Most states fall within a 3- to 7-year lookback period (three years is the most common). There is no national database—reports are obtained through a state's DMV or equivalent agency.

### What if someone has lived in multiple states?
Since the Commercial Motor Vehicle Safety Act of 1986, each driver may only have one driver's license and one record. Some states carry over violations from a previous state; some do not.
