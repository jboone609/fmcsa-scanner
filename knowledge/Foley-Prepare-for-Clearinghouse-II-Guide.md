## 1. The New FMCSA Drug & Alcohol Clearinghouse-II Regulation

### As of November 18, 2024

State Driver Licensing Agencies (SDLAs) must remove the commercial driving privileges from the driver's license of any individual with a "prohibited" FMCSA Clearinghouse status until the driver completes the return-to-duty (RTD) process.

**This means any drivers with unresolved FMCSA drug and/or alcohol Clearinghouse violations will lose or be denied the use of their CDLs or CLPs.**

Some SDLAs may begin the process prior to the November 18 deadline if they have the legislative authority to query the FMCSA Clearinghouse and act upon their findings.

If a driver is "prohibited" in the FMCSA Clearinghouse or faces a license downgrade, they must complete the DOT return-to-duty process involving evaluation, treatment, and testing, managed by a professional, to regain their commercial driving privileges.

**Now is the time to ensure your drivers will not be impacted by this regulation.**

## 2. Ensure Your DOT Drug & Alcohol Testing Program Is Compliant

According to federal law, all CDL drivers considered "safety-sensitive" employees must participate in a DOT-compliant drug and alcohol testing program. This includes drivers who:

- Operate vehicles weighing at least 26,001 pounds
- Transport hazardous materials
- Transport vehicles with at least 16 passengers

To ensure full compliance, all drivers listed above must participate in both a comprehensive drug and alcohol testing program **and** the FMCSA Drug and Alcohol Clearinghouse.

### Comprehensive DOT Drug & Alcohol Testing Programs Must Include:

- Pre-employment testing
- Post-accident testing
- Random testing
- Reasonable suspicion testing
- Return-to-duty/follow-up testing

Violations of any of the above — such as positive DOT drug test results or test refusals — must then be reported to the FMCSA Clearinghouse.

## 3. Enroll "Prohibited" Drivers in the DOT Return-to-Duty Process

If you run a query on any of your drivers and reveal they have a record in the Clearinghouse, they must be immediately removed from their safety-sensitive duties, including driving, and complete the return-to-duty (RTD) process before they can return to these responsibilities.

### The Return-to-Duty Process (Step by Step)

**Step 1 — Removal from safety-sensitive functions immediately:** The driver must stop driving as soon as it's safe to do so.

**Step 2 — Initial evaluation with a DOT-qualified Substance Abuse Professional (SAP):** During this face-to-face interview, the SAP will evaluate the employee to understand their situation and usage history, and determine what type of activities or treatment the employee must undergo.

**Step 3 — Education and treatment program:** The SAP will prescribe a treatment program for the employee based on their initial interview.

**Step 4 — SAP follow-up evaluation:** After the employee has completed the SAP treatment program, another evaluation must take place with the same SAP. At that time, they will determine whether the employee may take an RTD test and resume safety-sensitive functions.

**Step 5 — Return-to-duty drug test:** Once the SAP confirms that the employee has completed all requirements, they'll recommend to the Designated Employer Representative (DER) that the employee take an RTD test. The collection of the specimen must be directly observed by an individual of the same sex.

**Step 6 — Returning to duty:** Once a negative DOT test result is received, the employee may resume their safety-sensitive functions, such as driving.

**Step 7 — Follow-up test:** The SAP will provide the DER with a schedule of follow-up tests.

**Step 8 — FMCSA Clearinghouse reporting:** Employers must report negative RTD test results to the Clearinghouse by the close of the third business day following the date on which the results were received.

## 4. Running Your FMCSA Drug & Alcohol Clearinghouse Queries

Employers are required to check the Clearinghouse once during the pre-employment process and at least once annually for the duration of each CDL driver's employment.

Running regular limited queries on each of your drivers allows you to become aware of any drug or alcohol violations reported to the Clearinghouse and reveal if they are in "prohibited" status.

### Limited Clearinghouse Queries

Limited queries only show that a violation record exists for a driver — it doesn't show the details of the violation(s).

Employers can use a blanket consent form to run these queries. This consent form can remain in effect for the duration of a driver's employment, allowing them to run limited queries on drivers as often as they'd like.

If a limited query shows that a record exists for a driver, then an employer is required to run a full query within 24 hours. If the driver doesn't consent to the query, they will need to be pulled from safety-sensitive duties.

### Full Clearinghouse Queries

A full Clearinghouse query shows all the details of the driver's record and their violations. A full query is part of the required DOT background checks when hiring a new CDL driver.

Employers must get the driver's consent each time they intend to run a full query on them in the Clearinghouse. Consent for full queries must be given electronically through the Clearinghouse portal.

## 5. Managing Your FMCSA Drug & Alcohol Clearinghouse Records

Companies must maintain a record of all FMCSA Clearinghouse queries, as well as a record of a signed consent form for each driver. This consent form gives employers the ability to run limited Clearinghouse queries on their drivers for the duration of their employment.

All records must be stored in a secure, easily accessible location so they're readily available upon request by a DOT auditor.

### Document Retention Requirements

| Document | Retention Period |
|---|---|
| Signed Limited Consent Forms | 3 Years |
| Pre-Employment Queries | 3 Years |
| Annual Queries | 3 Years |

It's best to keep these records in a digital format. Not only are paper files extremely inefficient, but maintaining and periodically self-auditing them for accuracy also takes a great deal of time and organization. Files can often be misplaced or accidentally destroyed, and it's easy to overlook missing documents and gloss over expiration dates. Any of these situations can result in DOT violations and fines.

Even if you have an up-to-date, well-organized, and regularly checked paper recordkeeping system, it can be time-consuming, stressful, and arduous to meet the digital requirements of an offsite audit. After reviewing and self-auditing all documents for compliance, you may need to scan and send them to an auditor electronically in just a few short days if you're called for an offsite audit.

## 6. Run MVR Checks on Drivers Regularly

As a DOT-regulated business, you're well-accustomed to the process of pulling annual MVRs for every driver you employ. You must do so to meet federal recordkeeping requirements.

The challenge: What happens when one of your drivers gets a DUI or commits a traffic or safety violation soon after you pull their annual report? Or if they have their license revoked due to the Clearinghouse-II regulation? Unless they voluntarily come clean with new information on infractions or violations, you'll be in the dark until the next annual review or audit.

**Running regular MVR checks on your drivers can help avoid surprise CDL/CPL revocations resulting from the Clearinghouse-II regulation.**

### How Does MVR Monitoring Work?

MVR Monitoring keeps tabs on driver's license records. When there is a change, employers are immediately alerted to any "hit" and receive the updated MVR for review.

These "hits" could be anything from accidents or DUIs to speeding tickets or other violations or convictions. They could also signify CDL suspensions or revocations, safety violations, or medical certificate downgrades. They include positive changes as well, such as when a driver renews or updates their CDL or medical certificate.
