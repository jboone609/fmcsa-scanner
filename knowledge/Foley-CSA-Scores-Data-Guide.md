## The FMCSA CSA Program & CSA Scores

The CSA program (Compliance, Safety, Accountability) is designed to improve the safety of all drivers on the road. Each motor carrier has a CSA score that represents how well they meet the established requirements.

The CSA program launched in November 2010, and it is run by the FMCSA to encourage companies to be aware of their responsibility to keep our roadways safe.

**Each DOT-regulated company has a CSA score that represents how well they've maintained that road safety. The score is calculated on a 0–100 scale, with higher numbers indicating a worse level of safety. CSA scores are updated through the FMCSA's Safety Measurement System (SMS) each month.**

Since 2010, the CSA point system has gone through some changes to make the system fairer to drivers who were involved in accidents that were not their fault.

---

## How Your CSA Score Is Calculated

The core CSA metrics are called the **BASICS** (Behavior Analysis and Safety Improvement Categories). They're calculated using crash report and roadside inspection data collected in the FMCSA SMS, and will differ depending on factors like how long ago an incident occurred (only the last 24 months contribute to your score), the severity of a crash, and annual miles driven.

### FMCSA BASICS Overview

| BASIC Category | Description |
|---|---|
| **Crash Indicator** | The number, frequency, and severity of crashes. |
| **Controlled Substances and Alcohol** | Having a driver found to be operating a CMV under the influence. |
| **Driver Fitness** | Having a driver who has failed to maintain qualification files or is operating without a CDL. |
| **Hazardous Materials Compliance** | Improperly handling hazardous materials, such as failing to label materials or having leaky containers. |
| **Hours-of-Service Compliance** | As soon as your employees have driven for eight hours, they need to take a 30-minute break. They cannot drive their CMVs until after the 30 minutes have passed. |
| **Vehicle Maintenance** | Failing to maintain vehicles, such as faulty lights, brakes, or improper load securement. |
| **Unsafe Driving** | Speeding, improper lane changes, not wearing a seatbelt, and driving in a dangerous manner. |

## Deciphering Between Good & Bad CSA Scores

### What is a good CSA score?

The closer you can get your CSA score to 0, the better. If you have a good CSA score you may:

- Reduce your chances of being selected for DOT audits
- Benefit from lower insurance premiums
- Find it easier to land more clients as a responsible and trusted carrier

### What is a bad CSA score?

Generally, any score that's **50 or over** needs to be taken seriously.

- A score of **65% or higher** in Crash Indicator, HOS Compliance, or Unsafe Driving will subject you to an FMCSA investigation (lowers to 50% if you transport passengers or hazardous materials).
- A score of **80% or higher** in any of the other BASIC categories will also subject you to an investigation.

> **Reminder:** Drivers do not get individual CSA scores — only companies do. But if you're an owner-operator, your CSA score will reflect your driving and safety habits, as well as those of any other drivers for your company.

## How DOT Violations Impact CSA Scores

Any DOT violation received by your drivers or company remains on your record for **two years**. If your CSA score becomes too high, the FMCSA may decide to give you a compliance review. If your score suggests you are a danger on the road, an out-of-service order may be issued (only after multiple violations and warning letters).

## The Connection Between CSA Scores & Insurance Premiums

Most insurance underwriters use CAB Reports to determine their pricing decisions. **Your company's CSA score can influence your insurance premiums — for better or for worse. High scores can mean high premiums; low or zero scores mean more appealing premiums.**

Unless the company and agent can show the underwriter that their risk mitigation protocols are working (using hard data), the CAB Report will become the default — and the underwriter won't always get the whole story.

### Using Technology to Tell the Safety Story

Fleet owners and safety managers can now better monitor driver behavior and CSA scores, shifting from a reactive environment to a more proactive one. Recommendations:

- **Install telematics**, like cameras inside the cab, to monitor driver behavior.
- **Monitor the results** of poor driving, poor maintenance, or poor paperwork — take corrective action and document those actions to present to underwriters.

## How to Improve Your CSA Score

It's best to approach your CSA score proactively — preventing incidents rather than reacting when something bad happens.

### Hire Drivers Carefully
Balance getting trucks on the road with making sure your drivers don't have red flags that may cause your score to suffer. Run background checks before hiring.

### Train Drivers Properly & Regularly
Make sure your drivers understand how to handle difficult situations. Review any incidents regularly and provide safety reminders to keep them top of mind.

### Maintain Company Vehicles
Drivers should report any vehicle issues requiring repairs immediately. Open communication on mechanical and safety issues allows everyone to work toward a better CSA score.

### Complete Pre-Trip Checks
Remind drivers to check vehicles before leaving for their next run. This can help ensure they and the company aren't cited for violations that could have been easily prevented, like a faulty taillight.
